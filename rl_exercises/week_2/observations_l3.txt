Our Contextual marsROver has four different context features:
    "slip_termination_probability": 0.2,
    "slip_penalty": -20.0,
    "left_reward": 3.0,
    "right_reward": 10.0,

But only slip_termination_probability and right_reward are currently changed over different runs.
The idea is that the right state is terminal and has the higher reward but taking the action right is more risky then going for the safe(r) choice of taking the lower reward in the leftmost terminal state.
If the agent "slips" the episode terminates with a negative reward.
We use validation to test interpolation and test to test extrapolation and tried 3 different train sets (modes a to c from section 6.4).

Interpretation
--------------
With visible context, the train return improved for all modes. 
    This is because the agent can condition its policy on the context, instead of averaging over 
    different environments. This matches the paper’s finding that context-conditioned policies can 
    learn more discriminative behavior and achieve higher performance, since they can adapt to 
    each specific environment instance.

The test return greatly improved for modes A and C. For those modes hidden context performed badly.
    In modes A and C, different contexts require different optimal actions (e.g., going right is 
    sometimes worth the risk, sometimes not). A hidden-context policy must commit to a single 
    compromise strategy, which fails especially under distribution shift (test). Visible context 
    resolves this by enabling different actions per context. This directly reflects the paper’s 
    result that a single hidden-context policy cannot be optimal when contexts 
    induce conflicting optimal actions, leading to large performance drops without context.

In most cases, validation performed (much) better than test.
    Validation corresponds to interpolation (contexts within or close to the training distribution), 
    whereas test includes extrapolation (contexts outside the training range). 
    Agents generalize better within the training distribution but struggle outside of it. 
    This aligns with the paper’s experiments, where interpolation is significantly easier than 
    out-of-distribution generalization, and performance drops as the test contexts move further away 
    from the training distribution.

Our results compared to Figure 9 in the paper:
    mode A: 
        We saw that visible context performed much better than hidden context.
        In the paper there was not much of a difference.
    mode B: 
        We didn't see much of a difference between hidden and visible context. 
        In the paper visible context performed even worse than hidden context.
    mode C:
        We saw that visible context performed much better than hidden context.
        Similar results were also observed in the paper.
